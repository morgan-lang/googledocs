Creating and sharing beautiful, image-rich documents in Google Docs

You have probably heard of cloud computing at some point. It is a bit of a buzzword, but many people do not understand that they may already be using cloud computing. Google Docs was the first cloud computing service to achieve widespread acceptance among the public. As a SaaS application (SaaS is another buzzword term meaning Software as a Service), it offers many advantages over traditional software installations. It doesn’t offer all of the text-manipulation features of an application like Microsoft Word, yet it does provide a rich toolset that is probably good enough for most word processing tasks. In fact, this document was created using Google Docs.

All that said, let’s get started with how to create and share a document using Google Docs.

  1. Sign in to Google Docs.

Right away, we can see that there’s a difference between Google Docs and traditional word processors. Why do you need to sign in? Because, remember, Google Docs is SaaS, not software residing on your computer. When you use Google Docs, you interact with software on Google’s servers, using your browser as an interface.

Obviously, we will need an account with Google first. Whether you have a Google account or not, begin by opening Firefox or Chrome and entering the following web address:

Now you’re ready to begin working on your document. Things should look pretty familiar to people accustomed to Microsoft Word. We see that there are tools for invoking styles, font tools, alignment tools, and a number of other common tools. Since this tutorial presumes familiarity with word processing conventions, we will not address the fundamentals of composing a document. We will, however, point out some things that set Docs apart from word processors you may have used previously. Fonts and sharing/collaboration.

Fonts: Google Docs draws on an extensive library of open-source fonts. These fonts are extremely popular and Google’s font libraries are often drawn upon by web designers. Chances are good that you’ve seen at least one Google font (and probably dozens) while browsing the web today. Instead of downloading and installing fonts to your computer, you can use the fonts tool in Docs to import desired fonts from Google’s libraries.

Selecting More Fonts brings up the fonts browser.

Here, you can search for fonts or browse font examples. Because there are so many fonts available, it is a good idea to narrow field according to our interests. For example, if we’re only interested in sans serif fonts (most useful for headings and titles, less useful for large bodies of text), we would click the Show: All fonts button and select the category Sans Serif.

Let’s say we want a futuristic-looking font. We scroll down the list of fonts and notice one named Orbitron that seems to suit our needs. To add that font to your list of fonts, click it. A check mark will appear next to the font, indicating that it is ready to be imported into your font list. If you wish, you can select multiple fonts the same way at this point. When you’re done, click the OK button to return to your document and start using your new fonts.

Sharing/collaboration: finally we get to one of the major differences between Docs and traditional word processors: the ability to chare your document with others. Because Google docs reside in folders in Google’s cloud, you can provide people with access to those documents and even collaborate in real-time with others working on the same document. This is handy for group projects.

Before we begin sharing a document, it’s important to understand that your document is constantly being saved. It is not necessary to use command + S or to select Save from the File menu to save a document, and in fact those options aren’t available in Docs. This may make you uncomfortable, so Google has placed a reassuring message at the top of your document: All Changes Saved in Drive. What is Drive? It’s the online virtual hard drive that was created when you created your account. You will use it to store all your Docs, and you can also use it as a general-purpose repository, the same way you might use a hard drive on your computer. The difference is that your Google Drive is available anywhere you have a computer or mobile device with an internet connection.

While you make changes to a document, you will see the All changes saved in Drive message disappear briefly. This indicates that your changes are being tracked and saved. When the message returns, the changes are permanently stored in Drive unless you specifically undo them.

Adding and resizing images to Google Docs.

First, place your cursor at the place in the document where you want your image to appear. Then click on the Insert menu, then select Image.

 The Insert Image dialog will open. The default action you’re presented with here is to either drag an image into the document or navigate to the image’s location on your computer. You may find one method easier than another. In this tutorial, we’ll drag our image into the window.

Side note: there are several other options available through this interface, including linking to externally-hosted images and capturing an image through your webcam. We encourage you to explore these other methods of integrating images into your document once we’re done.

On your computer, locate an image you’d like to embed in your document, then drag it into the Insert Image window. Below, we’ve selected the image “wyoming-evening.jpg” and are about to drag it into the Insert Image window.

As soon as you drag your image into the Insert Image window, your image will be uploaded to Google Docs and embedded in your document. Notice that the image will be resized to automatically fit within your document’s margins.

Suppose you don’t want your image to take up so much space in your document. Fortunately, you can resize images after they have been embedded in your document. You can also change the way text flows around your images.. Let’s add some more text to your document, then resize the image, change the way nearby text flows around our image.

Begin by adding a paragraph of text below your image. In the example below, we’ve added dummy text generated by http://www.lipsum.com/ .

Once you’ve added your text, click once on your image. You will notice that a border appears around your image, and that the border is intersected by eight small squares: one at each corner and one in the middle of each side. Additionally, a line extends up from the top center of the image and is connected to a dot. What are these squares and dot for?

The squares are for resizing the image in different ways, and the dot is for rotating the image. To resize your image while keeping the proportions of the image the same, we’ll use the corner squares. To resize the image without keeping the proportions the same, we’ll use the side squares. Let’s start with the most common task: changing the size of the image while retaining the proportions of the original image. Click on any corner square and drag that square toward the center of the image. As you do this, the border around the image will shrink, indicating the target size of the image. Let’s drag our corner square until we have selected a target size about 25% of our original size. The width of our resized image will take up about half of the width it used to. Note that because our image, like our text, is left-aligned by default, it sits on the left side of the page. If we want it to be in the center of the page, click the undo button and select center alignment.

Note: don’t forget about the undo function!

Now we’ll change the way text flows around the image to give it a more magazine-like appearance. Note that below our selected image, there are three ways to get our image to interact with surrounding text: In line, Wrap text, and Break text. By default, Images are placed “In line,” meaning that they take up the entire vertical area “in line” with their height, preventing text from being placed next to them. To allow text to flow around our image, we’ll select Wrap text. Note that text now flows to the right of our image in the empty space we created when we resized our image.

So far, we’ve covered how to get started with Google Docs, including getting a Google account, creating a document, importing and using fonts, and embedding and editing images. These are the foundations upon which you can build your own skill set with Docs.

The next step in using Google Docs is sharing your document with others. Giving others access to your live documents is one of the most powerful parts of Google Docs. Remember when you had to email versions of your documents as attachments to emails? Those days are over, thanks to cloud sharing.

By default, documents are not shared with anyone and are completely private. To share your document with others, click the Share button in the upper right corner.

Clicking the Share button will bring up the sharing interface. To give one or more people access to your document, type in a recipient’s email address, then press the return button. To add additional recipients, repeat that process.

Note that by default, people with whom you share your document will have “can edit” access, meaning they will be able to make changes to your document. To restrict their access to your document, click on the permissions button in the upper right corner and select Can View, as we’ve done below:

When you have designated your recipients and adjusted their permissions levels, click the Send button. Note that this button is not sending your document, but is sending a link to your document residing on Google Drive.

Your recipients will receive an email that looks like this, allowing them to follow an embedded link to your document.

Important: although it is possible to share Docs with people who do not have Google accounts, it is a security risk to do so and is not recommended.

As you can see, Google Docs isn’t just a text editor. It is also capable of basic layout and image editing functions, and its greatest advantage over traditional word processing applications may be its sharing function. Now that you have acquired the basic skills necessary to get started with Google Docs and sharing, start experimenting with some of the many additional features of Docs.
